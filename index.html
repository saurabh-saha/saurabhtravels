Trip 1 · A note to myself

Why the break before the beginning mattered

Bengaluru ↔ Siliguri · ~4,900 km round trip · written 17 months later

Last February, in the gap before my first day of work, the three of us — me, my wife, and Cooper with his head permanently out the rear window — pointed the i20 north and drove the entire eastern edge of India. Up the coast past Chennai, Vijayawada, Visakhapatnam, Bhubaneswar, through Kolkata, and on to Siliguri under the shadow of the Himalayas. Then, when it was time, the same road home in reverse.

People asked why we didn't just fly. It's two and a half hours. The honest answer: I didn't want to teleport between two lives. I wanted the transition to take as long as it actually felt. (Also, nobody consulted Cooper, and Cooper does not fly.)

A break isn't the absence of progress. It's the part where progress gets absorbed.

What the road taught me about breaks

Ninety-odd hours of driving, split between two legs, gives your mind nowhere to hide. Somewhere along the Odisha coast the mental noise — packing, goodbyes, the offer letter, what-ifs — finally went quiet, and underneath it was space. Space to feel proud of what was ending instead of only anxious about what was next. Breaks do that. They convert experience into understanding. You don't process a whole chapter of your life while forwarding your mail. You process it at 80 km/h with the sea on your left, your wife picking the music, and a golden retriever snoring on the back seat.

And doing the same road twice turned out to be the point, not the flaw. On the way up it was anticipation; on the way back, the same towns and the same toll booths felt completely different, because we were different. Same route, new people driving it.

What I want to do next

It's been about a year and a half since that loop, and a full year of work is behind me. A lot happened — the kind of a lot that accumulates quietly while you're busy being reliable. Wins I didn't stop to celebrate, mistakes I didn't stop to forgive.

So the next trip isn't about distance. It's about finding a safe place to think — somewhere slow and quiet where I can lay out the last year the way I once laid out that route, and actually look at it. The hills, probably; the mountains we drove toward that February feel like the right place to return to. A week, a notebook, no itinerary. Cooper is already on board. He's always on board.

Because if the Big Loop taught me one thing, it's this: the pause isn't a detour from the journey. Sometimes it's the most important stretch of road on the whole map.

— Saurabh

About this page

This is my personal journey journal. Every trip here is a real drive — the routes, the cities, the crew in the car. I started driving in 2024 and haven't really stopped since; the plan, loosely, is to explore the length and breadth of India one highway at a time.

I live in Bengaluru with my wife and our two dogs — Cooper, a golden retriever who has seen more of India's coastline than most people, and Teddy, a poodle who mostly supervises from home.

I'm a web developer by profession. Driving is my release — the one place where the only thing that needs debugging is the playlist.
